⠿ The Problem

Blind students hunger to read.
The shelves just don't speak their language.

285M

people globally live with visual impairment — 19 million of them are children under 14 (NIH, 2023).

<10%

of all published books are ever converted into any accessible format. Braille fiction for children is a fraction of that.

~5M

Braille readers exist globally, yet most blind school libraries carry fewer than 20 story books in Braille.

⠂ What's Missing
  • Most Braille books in Indian blind schools are decades-old textbooks or religious scriptures — not stories children choose to read.
  • Reading is treated as academic tool for blind students — never as pleasure, never as escape, never as adventure.
  • Fiction and short stories in Braille are seen as "non-essential" — the first thing cut from budgets, the last thing donated.
  • Students frequently share one Braille volume among an entire class, or depend entirely on a teacher reading aloud.
  • Accessible formats are created only after a sighted book succeeds — Braille comes last, if at all.
  • This is not a capability problem. Blind children can and do read. They simply have nothing to read.
⠿ Our Answer

Braille readers process around 125 words per minute — a speed that matches spoken word and lets readers experience the flow of a real story, uninterrupted.

Common Questions

How do I know my contribution actually reached a student?+
Every distribution is documented — we collect acknowledgement letters from school principals, photograph the book delivery, and follow up with teacher feedback within 4–6 weeks. All sponsors and partners receive this documentation as part of their impact report.
Are contributions tax-deductible?+
We are working toward formal registration under Section 12A / 80G of the Indian Income Tax Act, which will make contributions eligible for tax exemption. Please reach out for the latest status — we are happy to share documentation of our current structure and registration timeline.
Can I specify which school receives the books I sponsor?+
Yes — Chapter and Patron sponsors may request distribution to a specific school or region, subject to our existing NGO network. We will confirm feasibility before accepting the commitment. Seed sponsors are allocated to the school most in need at that time.
What languages are the stories written in?+
Our initial collection is in English, written for English-medium blind schools. We are actively exploring Hindi and regional language editions — and would welcome partnerships with writers and transcribers who can support this expansion.
I'm a writer. Can I contribute without a financial commitment?+
Absolutely. All intellectual property rights remain entirely with you — we only request the right to transcribe and distribute the story in Braille format for non-commercial, educational purposes. Reach out through the form above and we'll share our content guidelines.

Adventure ⠿ 8 min read
The Lighthouse and the Storm
An Original Story — Stories Beyond Sight

The night Priya's grandfather first showed her the light-keeper's logbook, the storm had already swallowed the horizon. She could feel it through the lighthouse walls — not in any dramatic way, but in the way the old stone breathed differently when the sea was angry.

"Every ship out there," her grandfather said, not looking up from his instrument panel, "knows we are here because of this light. They cannot see the rocks. They can only see us."

Priya pressed her palm flat against the cold glass of the lantern room. Outside, the rain fell sideways. The beam swept its patient arc through the dark — once, twice, again — like a slow, luminous heartbeat refusing to stop.

"What if the light goes out?" she asked.

He turned then. His face, worn and warm, held no worry. "Then we use everything else we have," he said. "Our voice. Our bells. Our hands." He placed his wrinkled hand over hers on the glass. "The sea does not stop needing to be found just because the light is gone."

⠿ Title in Braille — "The Lighthouse"
⠿ Did You Know

A skilled Braille reader reads with both hands simultaneously — one hand reading ahead while the other follows. This makes the experience rhythmic, almost musical in its own way.

Slice of Life ⠿ 6 min read
Arjun's Orchestra
An Original Story — Stories Beyond Sight

Arjun had been collecting sounds since he was four years old. Not in jars or boxes — he had nowhere to put them — but in a long, unwritten list inside his head, ordered not by alphabet but by texture.

There were the smooth sounds: rain on a tin roof, his mother's bangles sliding down her wrist as she cooked, the hiss of a pressure cooker releasing steam. And then the rough ones: a bicycle chain skipping, the vegetable seller's cart on cobblestones, the crows arguing above the school's guava tree.

On Tuesday mornings, Arjun would sit at his classroom window and conduct them all. Not with a baton — just with his fingers, tapping the rhythm against his thigh. Nobody knew he was doing it. Nobody had to. The music was entirely, completely, precisely his.

When his teacher asked what he wanted to be when he grew up, he said, "A collector." She assumed he meant stamps. He didn't correct her.

⠿ Title in Braille — "Arjun"
⠿ Did You Know

Braille was originally based on a military code for reading messages at night without light. Louis Braille adapted it at the age of 15 — creating a system still in use 200 years later.

Friendship ⠿ 10 min read
The Girl Who Named the Wind
An Original Story — Stories Beyond Sight

Meera named the wind the same way other children named their pets: with great seriousness and without consulting anyone. The wind that came from the east — cool and a little apologetic — she called Neel. The hot, bossy one from the southwest she called Mira, after her least favourite aunt.

When the new girl arrived at school, she smelled of coconut oil and nervousness and something else — something Meera couldn't place. Like the air before the monsoon breaks.

"I'm Fatima," the girl said, in the particular way people introduce themselves when they're not sure they'll be remembered.

"I know," said Meera, though she didn't. "You smell like a new wind. I haven't named you yet."

Fatima went very quiet. Then she laughed — a sound that was, Meera decided, like the wind through bamboo. Bright and a little hollow and completely surprising.

"I'd like a good name," Fatima said carefully. "My last school gave me a bad one."

Meera considered this. "Tell me what direction you came from," she said, "and I'll decide."

⠿ Title in Braille — "Meera"
⠿ Did You Know

A single Braille page typically holds fewer words than a printed page. This means Braille books are physically larger — which is why short story formats are especially valuable for young readers beginning their Braille journey.
